Q1. When is activity not advised during pregnancy?
Some conditions require caution or avoidance of moderate-to-vigorous activity.

Relative contraindications (exercise with caution and medical discussion):
These include mild respiratory disorders, mild heart disease, well-controlled Type 1 diabetes, mild pre-eclampsia, placenta praevia after 28 weeks, untreated thyroid disease, eating disorders, undernutrition, and heavy smoking with other conditions.

Absolute contraindications (moderate-to-vigorous activity not recommended):
These include severe respiratory or heart disease, uncontrolled arrhythmia, placental abruption, vasa praevia, uncontrolled diabetes, intrauterine growth restriction, active preterm labour, severe pre-eclampsia, and cervical insufficiency.

If you have concerns, speak to a healthcare professional and complete the Get Active Questionnaire for Pregnancy (GAQ-P).

Q2. Will being active help with the birth?
It’s difficult to predict labour outcomes, but being active and healthy may support better recovery after birth.

Q3. I train regularly. Can I continue during pregnancy?
Yes, but activities may need adapting as pregnancy progresses. Contact sports, horse riding, sky diving, and scuba diving are not recommended. Rest and recovery are also important.

Q4. Will being active cause miscarriage or stillbirth?
No evidence shows that moderate physical activity increases the risk of miscarriage or stillbirth. Activity can benefit both physical and mental health.

Q5. I have morning sickness. Should I still be active?
If you feel well enough and your pregnancy is uncomplicated, light activity such as walking and fresh air may help.

Q6. I’m pregnant and have a disability. What should I do?
Activity should be adapted to your abilities. Any movement is better than none. Choose moderate activity where you can talk but not sing, adapt as pregnancy progresses, and return gradually after birth.
