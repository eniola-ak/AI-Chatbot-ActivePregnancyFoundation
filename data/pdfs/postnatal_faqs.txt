Q1. How quickly can I be active after the birth of my baby?
It depends on your birth and how you feel.

If you’ve had a straightforward birth, you can start light activities such as walking, pelvic floor exercises, and deep stomach exercises when you feel ready. After a caesarean section, it’s important to take more time to recover and listen to your body.

After your 6–8 week postnatal check, aim to gradually build up to 150 minutes of moderate activity per week and include muscle-strengthening activities twice a week.

If you were active during pregnancy, reintroduce activities gradually, adapting them as needed. If you were not active, start slowly. Daily pelvic floor exercises are recommended as soon as you feel able.

Q2. When is activity not advised after having a baby?
There is no evidence that gradual return to moderate activity is harmful, but intense activity should be avoided if you experience symptoms such as:

Urinary or faecal incontinence

Urgency that is difficult to control

Heaviness or pressure in the pelvic area

Pain during intercourse

Abdominal muscle separation or weakness

Lower back or pelvic pain

If you experience these symptoms, seek advice from a healthcare professional or a Pelvic Health Physiotherapist.

Q3. Can I breastfeed and be active?
Yes. There is no evidence that being active affects breastfeeding. Wearing a supportive bra and using breast pads may help with comfort and leakage.

Q4. What is a postnatal check-up and should I have one?
A postnatal check-up usually takes place 6–8 weeks after birth. It allows a healthcare professional to assess recovery and discuss physical activity, pelvic floor health, and overall wellbeing.
